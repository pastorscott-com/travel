There’s an interesting thread on the Escapees Forum<\/a> about backing a 5th wheel. \u00a0Here’s a synopsis along with some of my own thoughts on the topic.<\/p>\n

    \n
  1. If possible, always back from the driver’s side<\/strong> – doing it the other way puts more pressure on the spotter to give good directions<\/li>\n
  2. GOAL: Get out and look<\/strong> –\u00a0at the beginning of the backup for sure – then anytime you have any doubt whatsoever about where you are and what is behind you<\/li>\n
  3. Look up<\/strong> – don’t get so focused on the ground that you forget tree limbs, etc.<\/li>\n
  4. Your spotter needs to know that if they can’t see your face in the mirrors<\/strong> that you can’t see them\u00a0– if you lose them, stop and wait until you can see them again.<\/li>\n
  5. Your spotter’s responsibility is what is happening at\u00a0the rear of the rig, the driver is responsible<\/strong> of that is happening at the nose of the pickup – watch out for other vehicles, etc. as you swing the nose of the pickup around to line up the 5th wheel.<\/li>\n
  6. Remember that the “superior” people who seem to be the most entertained by your backing in are likely some of the worse drivers<\/strong> in the campground and need you to have problems to make them feel better about their own shortcomings (after all the worse golfer in the foursome is everyone else’s best friend).<\/li>\n
  7. Pull forward<\/strong>\u00a0far enough – with my shortbed\/slider hitch combination that’s putting the rear of the 5th wheel 5 to 10 feet past the campsite<\/li>\n
  8. Watch the camper tires<\/strong> and turn just tight enough for them to clear the edge of the campsite driveway as you begin backing into the site<\/li>\n
  9. Once you get the tires on the side you are backing from into the edge of the campsite stop turning and start “following”<\/strong> the camper into the site with the pickup – imagine the camper is pulling the pickup<\/li>\n<\/ol>\n
